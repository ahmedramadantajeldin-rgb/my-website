Q1. How do you classify materials for engineering use?
Given: Conceptual / definition question — no numerical data  |  Required: A systematic classification of engineering materials
Solution

Engineering materials are broadly classified into the following major groups:

1. Metals and Alloys
  • Ferrous Metals — Iron and its alloys (cast iron, wrought iron, steel). These are magnetic, have high strength and are the most widely used structural metals.
  • Non-Ferrous Metals — Metals that do not contain iron as the principal constituent (aluminium, copper, zinc, lead, tin, nickel, titanium, etc.).
2. Non-Metallic Materials
  • Ceramics — Inorganic, non-metallic solids such as glass, brick, abrasives, refractories, and cutting tool tips (Al₂O₃, SiC).
  • Polymers / Plastics — Long-chain organic molecules, either thermoplastic (nylon, polyethylene, PVC) or thermosetting (bakelite, epoxy, polyester).
  • Composites — Two or more materials combined to obtain superior properties (GFRP, CFRP, concrete).
  • Elastomers / Rubber — Natural or synthetic polymers with high elastic deformation (tyres, seals, gaskets).
3. Electronic / Semiconducting Materials
  • Materials whose electrical conductivity lies between conductors and insulators (silicon, germanium, GaAs).
✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Engineering materials are classified as: (i) Metals & Alloys — Ferrous (Fe-based) and Non-Ferrous; (ii) Ceramics; (iii) Polymers / Plastics; (iv) Composites; and (v) Semiconductors / Electronic materials.
Q2. What are the factors to be considered for the selection of materials for the design of machine elements?
Required: Enumerate and explain the key selection factors
Solution

The selection of an appropriate material for a machine element involves the following factors:

  • 1. Mechanical Properties — Strength (tensile, compressive, shear), hardness, toughness, fatigue strength, creep resistance, and ductility, must match the service loads.
  • 2. Manufacturing / Processing Considerations — Machinability, weldability, castability, formability, and heat-treatability determine ease of production.
  • 3. Reliability and Service Conditions — Operating temperature range, exposure to corrosive media, cyclic loading (fatigue life), and contact stresses.
  • 4. Availability — The material must be commercially available in the required form (bar, sheet, tube) and in sufficient quantity.
  • 5. Cost — Initial material cost plus manufacturing cost (machining, heat treatment, surface finishing) must be economically justifiable.
  • 6. Weight / Density — Critical for aerospace, automotive and portable equipment where weight reduction is important.
  • 7. Corrosion and Wear Resistance — Surface degradation by chemical attack or abrasion must be resisted.
  • 8. Aesthetic and Special Requirements — Colour, surface finish, electrical/thermal conductivity, magnetic properties, etc.
  • 9. Environmental / Sustainability Concerns — Recyclability, toxicity and environmental impact.
✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Key selection factors: mechanical properties, manufacturability, reliability, availability, cost, weight, corrosion/wear resistance, and special functional requirements.

Definition of Mechanical Property

A mechanical property is a characteristic that describes the behaviour of a material when subjected to external forces or loads. It defines the relationship between the applied force (stress) and the resulting deformation (strain).

Q5. Define the following properties of a material: (i) Ductility, (ii) Toughness, (iii) Hardness, and (iv) Creep.
Required: Precise engineering definitions of four mechanical properties
(i) Ductility

Ductility is the ability of a material to undergo significant plastic (permanent) deformation before fracture when subjected to a tensile load. It is quantified by:

  • Percentage Elongation (%EL) = [(Lf − L₀) / L₀] × 100
  • Percentage Reduction in Area (%RA) = [(A₀ − Af) / A₀] × 100

A material is considered ductile if %EL > 5%. Ductile materials give warning before fracture (necking is visible). Example: Mild steel, copper, aluminium.

(ii) Toughness

Toughness is the ability of a material to absorb energy up to fracture. It is represented by the total area under the engineering stress-strain curve and is expressed in J/m³.

Notch toughness is measured by the Charpy or Izod impact test (energy in Joules). Toughness requires both strength AND ductility. Example: Manganese steel, austenitic stainless steel.

(iii) Hardness

Hardness is the resistance of a material to localised surface deformation such as indentation, scratching, or abrasion. It is NOT a single fundamental property but a composite of strength and ductility.

Common hardness scales: Brinell (BHN) — steel ball indenter; Vickers (HV) — diamond pyramid; Rockwell (HRC/HRB) — diamond cone or ball; Mohs — scratch resistance (minerals). Example: White cast iron (HRC ≈ 55–65), hardened die steel.

(iv) Creep

Creep is the progressive, time-dependent plastic deformation of a material under a constant applied stress at elevated temperature (typically T > 0.4 Tm, where Tm is the melting point in Kelvin). Creep behaviour has three stages:

  • Primary (Transient) Creep — decreasing strain rate as the material work-hardens.
  • Secondary (Steady-State) Creep — nearly constant (minimum) strain rate; most design calculations are based on this stage.
  • Tertiary Creep — accelerating strain rate leading to necking and rupture.

Creep is critical for steam turbine blades, boiler tubes, jet engine components, and high-temperature pressure vessels. Example: Nickel superalloys are designed to resist creep at 800–1100 °C.

✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Ductility = large plastic deformation before fracture; Toughness = energy absorption before fracture; Hardness = resistance to surface indentation; Creep = time-dependent plastic flow under constant stress at high temperature.
Q6. How cast iron is obtained? Classify and explain different types of cast irons.
Required: Production method + classification of cast irons
How Cast Iron is Obtained

Cast iron is produced by re-melting pig iron (from the blast furnace) together with scrap steel and/or cast iron in a cupola furnace, an electric arc furnace, or an induction furnace. Carbon content is 2–4% C, with 1–3% Si and other alloying/tramp elements. The melt is then cast into moulds; the cooling rate and composition determine the microstructure and type of cast iron formed.

Classification and Types
  • 1. Grey Cast Iron — Carbon precipitates as flake graphite during slow cooling. The flakes cause the fractured surface to appear grey. Properties: high compressive strength, excellent machinability, good damping capacity, brittle in tension. IS Grade: FG200 (UTS = 200 N/mm²). Applications: machine tool beds, engine blocks, cylinder liners, pipes.
  • 2. White Cast Iron — Rapid cooling prevents graphite formation; carbon remains as cementite (Fe₃C). Extremely hard (HRC 65+) and brittle; difficult to machine. Used as an intermediate product for malleable iron production, or for wear-resistant liners and rolls.
  • 3. Malleable Cast Iron — White cast iron is annealed (850–1000 °C for 30–150 hrs); cementite decomposes to temper carbon nodules (irregular graphite). Types: whiteheart, blackheart, and pearlitic malleable iron. Better ductility than grey iron. Applications: pipe fittings, brackets, hubs.
  • 4. Spheroidal Graphite (SG) / Nodular / Ductile Cast Iron — Magnesium (or cerium) is added to the melt before casting; graphite solidifies as spheres rather than flakes. This eliminates the notch effect of flakes, giving high tensile strength (400–900 MPa), good ductility (%EL = 2–18%) and toughness. Applications: crankshafts, gears, pressure pipes.
  • 5. Compacted Graphite Iron (CGI) — Graphite morphology between flake and spheroidal; higher thermal conductivity than SG iron. Used for diesel engine cylinder blocks.
  • 6. Alloy Cast Iron — Cast iron with added alloying elements (Ni, Cr, Mo, Cu) to improve mechanical properties or corrosion/heat resistance. Examples: Ni-resist (corrosion resistant), austenitic cast irons.
✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Six types: Grey, White, Malleable, Ductile (SG/Nodular), Compacted Graphite, and Alloy Cast Iron — differentiated by graphite morphology (flake → spheroid) controlled by composition and cooling rate.

Q9. Define alloy steel. Discuss the effects of nickel, chromium and manganese on steel.
Required: Definition of alloy steel + effects of Ni, Cr, Mn
Definition

Alloy steel is a steel to which one or more alloying elements other than carbon have been intentionally added in amounts sufficient to achieve specific improvements in mechanical, physical, or chemical properties. Common alloying elements include Ni, Cr, Mn, Mo, W, V, Si, Co, and Cu. The alloy content is typically 1–50% by mass.

Effect of Nickel (Ni)
  • Dissolves in ferrite, increasing strength and toughness without reducing ductility.
  • Lowers the eutectoid transformation temperature, refining grain size.
  • Improves corrosion and oxidation resistance (especially with Cr).
  • Increases hardenability — greater depth of hardening during quenching.
  • Improves toughness at sub-zero temperatures (used in cryogenic equipment).
  • Example alloy: 2.5% Ni steel for heavy-duty gears and shafts.
Effect of Chromium (Cr)
  • Forms hard chromium carbides (Cr₂₃C₆, Cr₇C₃) — improves hardness and wear resistance.
  • Raises the eutectoid temperature and stabilises carbides, improving hot strength.
  • At ≥ 12% Cr, forms a passive oxide film (Cr₂O₃) — gives stainless steel its corrosion resistance.
  • Increases hardenability and improves high-temperature oxidation resistance.
  • Example alloy: 18% Cr – 8% Ni stainless steel (Type 304).
Effect of Manganese (Mn)
  • Deoxidiser and desulphuriser — combines with sulphur to form MnS, preventing hot shortness.
  • Increases tensile strength and hardness without greatly affecting ductility.
  • Stabilises austenite — at 12–14% Mn (Hadfield steel) the austenite is retained at room temperature; the steel is tough, non-magnetic, and highly work-hardening.
  • Improves hardenability (depth of hardening).
  • Example alloy: Hadfield manganese steel for rock-crushing jaws and railway crossings.
✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Nickel → toughness, hardenability, low-T impact strength. Chromium → hardness, wear resistance, corrosion resistance (stainless). Manganese → deoxidation, strength, work-hardening in high-Mn grades.

Q12. Write short notes on free cutting steel, and stainless steel.
Required: Short notes on two specific steel types
(a) Free Cutting Steel

Free cutting steel (also called free machining steel) is a type of steel that has been designed for high-speed machining with minimum tool wear. This is achieved by adding one or more of the following elements:

  • Sulphur (S) 0.10–0.35% — Forms MnS inclusions that act as chip breakers, producing short chips and reducing friction on the tool face.
  • Lead (Pb) 0.15–0.35% — Dispersed as fine metallic droplets; acts as a solid lubricant at the tool-workpiece interface.
  • Phosphorus (P) up to 0.12% — Increases the brittleness of chips.
  • Bismuth / Tellurium — Modern lead-free alternatives.

Key Properties: Excellent machinability (2–3× faster cutting speeds), good surface finish, easy chip disposal, lower tool wear.

Limitations: Lower ductility, toughness, and weldability compared to equivalent plain carbon steels. Lead-containing grades raise environmental concerns.

IS/AISI Grade: AISI 1215 (0.09%C, 0.27%S, 0.09%P). Applications: Mass-produced turned parts — bolts, screws, studs, fittings on automatic screw machines.

(b) Stainless Steel

Stainless steel is an alloy steel containing a minimum of 10.5% chromium (typically 12–30% Cr), which forms a tenacious, self-healing passive film of Cr₂O₃ on the surface, providing corrosion resistance.

Main Types:

  • Austenitic (300 series, e.g. AISI 304 — 18% Cr, 8% Ni): Most widely used; excellent corrosion resistance, non-magnetic, not hardenable by heat treatment but work-hardens rapidly. Applications: food processing, chemical plants, kitchen equipment.
  • Ferritic (400 series, e.g. AISI 430 — 17% Cr): Moderate corrosion resistance, magnetic, not heat-treatable. Applications: automotive trim, kitchen appliances.
  • Martensitic (e.g. AISI 410 — 12% Cr, 0.15%C): Hardenable by quenching, moderate corrosion resistance, high strength. Applications: turbine blades, surgical instruments, cutlery.
  • Duplex (e.g. 2205 — 22% Cr, 5% Ni, 3% Mo): Mixed austenite-ferrite; very high strength and better stress-corrosion cracking resistance. Applications: offshore oil & gas, chemical plant.
  • Precipitation-Hardening (e.g. 17-4 PH): Highest strength among stainless steels; hardened by ageing. Applications: aerospace structural parts.
✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Free cutting steel: S/Pb additions improve machinability for automatic machining. Stainless steel: ≥10.5% Cr provides passive film corrosion resistance; main types are austenitic (304), ferritic (430), and martensitic (410).
Q13. Select suitable material for the following cases, indicating the reason.
Required: Material selection with justification for 4 cases
1. A Shaft Subjected to Variable Torsional and Bending Load

Selected Material: Alloy Steel 40Cr4 (SAE 5140) or 40Ni2Cr1Mo28 — Medium carbon Cr-Mo alloy steel, oil-quenched and tempered.

Reasons: Variable loading induces fatigue; the shaft requires high fatigue strength (endurance limit ≈ 500–600 MPa), high torsional shear strength, and good impact toughness. The Cr-Mo addition increases hardenability, allowing through-hardening of large sections, and Mo prevents temper brittleness. Heat treatment (Q&T) gives UTS ≈ 900–1100 MPa with %EL ≈ 12–15%.

2. Spring Used in a Spring-Loaded Safety Valve

Selected Material: High-carbon spring steel (0.60–0.80% C), or chromium-vanadium spring steel (SAE 6150), hardened and tempered to a high yield strength.

Reasons: Springs require very high elastic limit (proof stress / yield strength) so they do not take permanent set under repeated deflection. Cr-V steel gives high fatigue strength (essential for cyclic operation), good toughness, and excellent spring characteristics. After oil-quenching and tempering at 420–480 °C, 0.2% proof stress ≈ 1400–1600 MPa.

3. Nut of a Heavy-Duty Screw Jack

Selected Material: Phosphor bronze (Cu–Sn–P alloy, e.g. SAE 65 — 90% Cu, 10% Sn) or Leaded bronze.

Reasons: The screw jack nut is in sliding contact with the steel screw; it must have: (i) low coefficient of friction against steel, (ii) good wear resistance to prevent galling, (iii) ability to embed dirt particles (conformability), and (iv) sufficient compressive strength. Bronze provides all of these at moderate cost.

4. Low-Speed Line-Shaft Coupling

Selected Material: Grey cast iron (IS FG 200) or Mild steel (IS:2062 Grade A).

Reasons: A low-speed coupling is not subjected to impact; it requires moderate strength, dimensional stability, ease of casting or machining to shape, and low cost. Grey CI provides good machinability, adequate strength (UTS 200 N/mm²), and damping to absorb torsional vibration. For higher torques, mild steel or medium carbon steel flanged couplings are preferred.

Q16. Explain the following heat treatment processes: 1. Normalising; 2. Hardening; and 3. Tempering.
Required: Definitions, procedures, microstructural changes, and purposes of three heat treatment processes
1. Normalising

Definition: Normalising is a heat treatment process in which steel is heated to 30–50 °C above the upper critical temperature (Ac₃ for hypoeutectoid steel, Acm for hypereutectoid steel), held at that temperature for sufficient time, and then cooled in still air at room temperature.

Typical temperature: 850–950 °C for plain carbon steels.

Microstructural outcome: Finer and more uniform pearlite + ferrite (or pearlite + cementite) microstructure compared to the as-cast or as-forged state.

Purposes:

  • Refine the grain structure distorted by forging, rolling, or welding.
  • Relieve internal stresses from casting or cold working.
  • Improve mechanical properties (strength and toughness) and machinability.
  • Prepare the steel for subsequent heat treatment (hardening).

Application example: Forgings, castings, cold-worked steels, and weldments are normalised before machining.

2. Hardening

Definition: Hardening is a process in which steel is heated to the austenitising temperature (Ac₃ + 30–50 °C for hypoeutectoid, Ac₁ + 30–50 °C for hypereutectoid steel), held long enough for complete austenitisation, and then rapidly quenched in a suitable medium (water, oil, brine, or polymer quenchant).

Microstructural outcome: The rapid quench suppresses diffusion and traps carbon in the FCC iron lattice; this transforms to a supersaturated body-centred tetragonal (BCT) structure called martensite, which is extremely hard and brittle.

Martensite hardness: Depends primarily on carbon content (HRC ≈ 38 at 0.2%C → 65 at 0.8%C).

Quenching media (in order of quench severity): Brine > Water > Polymer > Oil > Air.

Hardenability: The depth to which martensite is formed during quenching; improved by alloying elements (Cr, Ni, Mo, Mn, B).

Application example: Gears, ball bearing races, cutting tool blanks, springs.

3. Tempering

Definition: Tempering is a heat treatment performed on hardened (martensitic) steel by reheating it to a temperature below Ac₁ (typically 150–650 °C), holding at temperature, and then cooling (usually in air).

Purpose: As-quenched martensite is too brittle for practical use; tempering reduces brittleness and internal stresses at the expense of some hardness and strength.

Stages of Tempering
StageTemp. RangeChange
Stage I80–200 °CCarbon begins to segregate from BCT martensite; ε-carbide (Fe₂.₄C) precipitates → slight softening
Stage II200–300 °CRetained austenite transforms to bainite; reduced brittleness
Stage III300–450 °Cε-carbide transforms to Fe₃C (cementite); martensite → tempered martensite; significant toughness improvement
Stage IV450–650 °CCoarsening of Fe₃C; recovery and recrystallisation of matrix; near-equilibrium microstructure; high toughness, lower hardness

Effect on properties: As tempering temperature increases, hardness and yield strength decrease while ductility and toughness increase. The engineer selects the tempering temperature to give the desired property balance.

Common tempering practices:

  • Low temper (150–200 °C): Cutting tools, case-hardened parts — retain maximum hardness (HRC 60–65).
  • Medium temper (350–450 °C): Springs — high yield strength and elastic limit.
  • High temper / Quench and Temper (550–650 °C): Structural steels, shafts, gears — maximum toughness.
✔ KEY ANSWER / SUMMARY: Normalising: Heat above Ac₃ then air-cool → refine grain structure and relieve stresses. Hardening: Heat above Ac₃ then quench rapidly → martensite forms (hard & brittle). Tempering: Reheat hardened steel below Ac₁ → convert brittle martensite to tough tempered martensite at controlled hardness–toughness balance.
