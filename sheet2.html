01 What are fits and tolerances? How are they designated? Theory
Question
What are fits and tolerances? How are they designated?
Given & Required

Given

  • Conceptual/definitional question — no numerical data

Required

  • Definition of Tolerance
  • Definition of Fits (all types)
  • Designation system (IS/ISO)
Solution
Part A — Tolerance
Tolerance is the total permissible variation in the size of a manufactured part. It is the algebraic difference between the upper limit and the lower limit of size, and is always a positive quantity.
$$\text{Tolerance} = \text{Upper Limit} - \text{Lower Limit}$$ $$T = \text{ES} - \text{EI} \quad \text{(for holes)} \qquad T = \text{es} - \text{ei} \quad \text{(for shafts)}$$
where ES = Upper deviation of hole, EI = Lower deviation of hole, es = Upper deviation of shaft, ei = Lower deviation of shaft
Two types exist:
UNILATERAL
$$25^{\,+0.00}_{-0.04}\;\text{mm}$$
Variation permitted on one side only of basic size
BILATERAL
$$25 \pm 0.02\;\text{mm}$$
Variation permitted on both sides of basic size
Part B — Fits
A fit is the degree of tightness or looseness between two mating parts (hole and shaft) before assembly. It depends on the algebraic difference between the sizes of the hole and shaft.
Type of FitConditionClearanceApplication
Clearance FitHole always > ShaftAlways positive \((+)\)Running bearings, sliding parts
Interference FitShaft always > HoleAlways negative \((-)\)Permanent assemblies, press fits
Transition FitMay be eitherPositive or negativeAccurate location, keyed shafts
Part C — Designation System
According to IS:919 and ISO 286, fits are designated as:
$$\underbrace{50}_{\text{Basic Size (mm)}} \;\underbrace{H}_{\text{Hole letter (cap)}}\underbrace{8}_{\text{IT grade}} \;/\; \underbrace{f}_{\text{Shaft letter (lower)}}\underbrace{7}_{\text{IT grade}}$$
Example: 50 H8/f7 → Basic size = 50 mm | Hole = H8 | Shaft = f7
\(\text{Tolerance} = \text{UL} - \text{LL}\) (always positive)  |  Three fit types: Clearance \((+)\), Interference \((-)\), Transition \((\pm)\)  |  Designation: \(\text{BasicSize}\;\text{HoleLetter+ITGrade}/\text{ShaftLetter+ITGrade}\) e.g. \(50\,H8/f7\)
02 What do you understand by the nominal size and basic size? Theory
Question
What do you understand by the nominal size and basic size?
Solution
Nominal Size
The nominal size is the size used for the purpose of general identification in engineering drawings. It is an approximate or rounded value that serves as the general description of a dimension.
Basic Size
The basic size is the theoretical exact reference size from which all limits of size are derived by the application of upper and lower deviations.
$$\text{Upper Limit} = \text{Basic Size} + \underbrace{\text{ES}}_{\text{upper deviation (hole)}\text{ or } \text{es (shaft)}}$$ $$\text{Lower Limit} = \text{Basic Size} + \underbrace{\text{EI}}_{\text{lower deviation (hole)}\text{ or } \text{ei (shaft)}}$$
AspectNominal SizeBasic Size
PurposeGeneral identification / labellingReference for all tolerance calculations
PrecisionRounded or approximateExact theoretical value
UsageDrawing callouts, general specsDeviation and limit calculations
Example"25 mm shaft"25.000 mm (exact reference)
Nominal size = rounded/approximate dimension used for identification.
Basic size = exact reference dimension from which tolerances (upper and lower deviations) are applied to obtain limits of size. In practice, both are numerically equal.
03 Short notes on: (a) Interchangeability  (b) Tolerance  (c) Allowance  (d) Fits Theory
Question
Write short notes on: (a) Interchangeability   (b) Tolerance   (c) Allowance   (d) Fits
Solution
(a) Interchangeability
Interchangeability is the ability to select any part at random from a large batch and assemble it with its mating part without any fitting or modification, while still satisfying the functional requirements of the assembly. It is the fundamental principle behind mass production.
  • Reduces manufacturing cost through standardisation
  • Eliminates selective assembly and hand fitting
  • Facilitates rapid replacement of worn components
  • Relies on all parts being manufactured within specified tolerance limits
(b) Tolerance
Tolerance is the permissible variation in a dimension — the range within which the actual manufactured size must fall to be acceptable.
$$T_{\text{hole}} = \text{ES} - \text{EI} \qquad T_{\text{shaft}} = \text{es} - \text{ei}$$ $$\text{(Tolerance is always a positive quantity)}$$
Unilateral: All tolerance on one side —
\(25^{\,+0.00}_{-0.04}\) mm
Bilateral: Tolerance on both sides —
\(25 \pm 0.02\) mm
(c) Allowance
Allowance is the intentional, designed difference between the basic sizes of the mating hole and shaft. It represents the minimum clearance (positive) or maximum interference (negative) between two mating parts.
$$\text{Allowance} = \text{Min. Limit of Hole} - \text{Max. Limit of Shaft}$$ $$\text{Allowance} > 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Clearance Fit} \qquad \text{Allowance} < 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Interference Fit}$$
(d) Fits
A fit describes the relationship (tightness or looseness) between a shaft and a hole before assembly.
Fit TypeDefinitionClearanceExamples
ClearanceHole always larger than shaft\(\geq 0\) (positive)Journal bearings, running spindles
InterferenceShaft always larger than hole\(< 0\) (negative)Gear hubs, flywheel on crankshaft
TransitionEither clearance or interference\(+\) or \(-\)Keyed shafts, coupling hubs
Interchangeability → random assembly without fitting.  |  Tolerance \(= \text{UL} - \text{LL}\) (positive).  |  Allowance \(= \text{Min. Hole} - \text{Max. Shaft}\).  |  Fits: Clearance \((+)\), Interference \((-)\), Transition \((\pm)\).
04 Difference in assembly type: Running fits vs Interference fits Theory
Question
What is the difference in the type of assembly generally used in running fits and interference fits?
Solution
Running Fits (Clearance Fits)
In running fits, the hole is always larger than the shaft, guaranteeing positive clearance at all times. Assembly is achieved by hand or light push — no external force, heating, or cooling required.
$$\text{Min. Clearance} = \text{Min. Hole} - \text{Max. Shaft} > 0$$ $$\text{Max. Clearance} = \text{Max. Hole} - \text{Min. Shaft} > 0$$
  • Assembly method: Slip / push by hand
  • Relative motion: Rotation or sliding possible after assembly
  • Lubrication: Required to reduce friction and wear
  • Applications: Journal bearings, spindle bearings, sliding keys, piston–cylinder
Interference Fits
In interference fits, the shaft is always larger than the hole, producing negative clearance. Assembly requires significant external force or thermal methods.
$$\text{Interference} = \text{Max. Shaft} - \text{Min. Hole} > 0$$ $$\text{(negative clearance — shaft is larger)}$$
  • Press fitting: hydraulic or mechanical press applies axial force
  • Shrink fitting: heat the hub (hole part) → insert shaft → cool to grip
  • Expansion fitting: cool shaft in liquid nitrogen → insert → warm to grip
PropertyRunning Fit (Clearance)Interference Fit
Shaft vs HoleShaft < Hole alwaysShaft > Hole always
Assembly forceNone (hand assembly)Large force or thermal method
After assemblyRelative motion possiblePermanently locked — no motion
LubricationRequiredNot required
DisassemblyEasy (slip apart)Difficult (press or heat)
Running fits: slip/hand assembly, shaft < hole, lubrication needed, parts can move relative to each other.
Interference fits: press/shrink/expansion assembly, shaft > hole, parts permanently locked with no relative motion.
05 Unilateral System of Tolerances — Definition, Application, Advantages Theory
Question
State briefly the unilateral system of tolerances — definition, application, and advantages over bilateral.
Solution
Definition
In the unilateral system, the tolerance is allowed on one side only of the basic size. One limit equals the basic size exactly; all tolerance accumulates on the other side.
$$\text{Shaft (unilateral):}\quad 25^{\,+0.00}_{-0.04}\;\text{mm} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{Upper limit} = \text{Basic size; all tolerance below}$$ $$\text{Hole (unilateral):}\quad 25^{\,+0.04}_{-0.00}\;\text{mm} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{Lower limit} = \text{Basic size; all tolerance above}$$ $$\text{Bilateral (contrast):}\quad 25 \pm 0.02\;\text{mm} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{Tolerance on both sides}$$
Application
  • IS:919 / ISO 286 standard tolerance grades (IT grades) — all unilateral
  • Hole basis and shaft basis systems — both use unilateral tolerances
  • Mass production of bearings, gears, shafts, and precision components
  • Go / No-Go gauging systems
Advantages over Bilateral System
AdvantageUnilateralBilateral
Gauge designSimple — one Go, one No-Go limitMore complex gauges needed
Fit type controlChanging tolerance does NOT change fit typeFit type may change if tolerance is altered
Standard complianceIS / ISO recommendedNot standard in IS/ISO limit system
MachiningMaterial removed in one direction onlyBidirectional removal — harder to control
Mass productionEasier inspection and process controlLess convenient for large batches
Unilateral tolerance: all variation on one side of basic size \(\left(e.g.,\;25^{+0.00}_{-0.04}\right)\).
Preferred by IS/ISO. Advantages: simpler Go/No-Go gauges, fit type unchanged when tolerance value is adjusted, easier machining control and mass-production inspection.
06 Hole Basis System vs Shaft Basis System — Which is preferred? Theory
Question
What is meant by 'hole basis system' and 'shaft basis system'? Which one is preferred and why?
Solution
Hole Basis System
The hole size is kept constant; different fits are obtained by varying the shaft size. The fundamental deviation of the hole is zero — the lower limit equals the basic size.
$$\text{Hole symbol: } H \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{EI} = 0$$ $$\text{Lower Limit of Hole} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{EI} = \text{Basic Size}$$ $$\text{Upper Limit of Hole} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{ES} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{IT grade value}$$
Fit types achieved by shaft selection:
Clearance fits:
Shaft designations: a, b, c, d, e, f, g, h
Transition fits:
Shaft designations: j, k, m, n
Interference fits: Shaft designations: p, r, s, t, u, v, x, y, z
Shaft Basis System
The shaft size is kept constant; different fits are obtained by varying the hole size. The fundamental deviation of the shaft is zero — the upper limit equals the basic size.
$$\text{Shaft symbol: } h \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{es} = 0$$ $$\text{Upper Limit of Shaft} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{es} = \text{Basic Size}$$ $$\text{Lower Limit of Shaft} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{ei} = \text{Basic Size} - \text{IT grade value}$$
Which System is Preferred — and Why?
The Hole Basis System is universally preferred for the following engineering and economic reasons:
  • Hole manufacturing difficulty: Producing holes to precise dimensions is more difficult and costly than turning shafts
  • Fixed tooling for holes: Reamers, broaches, and drill bits are fixed-size — difficult to adjust
  • Shaft adjustability: Shaft diameter is easily adjusted by turning or grinding to any required size
  • Gauge economy: Fewer standard plug gauges needed for hole inspection in the hole basis system
  • IS:919 recommendation: Indian Standard explicitly recommends the hole basis (H) system
Hole basis: hole fixed \((\text{EI}=0,\;\text{symbol }H)\), shaft size varied for different fits.
Shaft basis: shaft fixed \((\text{es}=0,\;\text{symbol }h)\), hole size varied.
Hole basis system is preferred — holes are harder to adjust than shafts; more economical for mass production. IS:919 recommends hole basis.
07 Indian Standard System of Limits and Fits (IS:919) Theory
Question
Discuss the Indian standard system of limits and fits.
Solution
Overview
The Indian Standard system of limits and fits is codified in IS:919 (Part I) — 1993, harmonised with ISO 286. It provides a rational framework for specifying interchangeable parts in the range 1 – 500 mm.
Fundamental Tolerance Unit (\(i\))
The standard tolerance unit \(i\) (in microns, \(\mu\text{m}\)) for IT grades 5 to 7 is:
$$i\;(\mu\text{m}) = 0.45\,\sqrt[3]{D} + 0.001\,D$$ $$\text{where } D = \sqrt{D_1 \times D_2}\;\text{(geometric mean diameter of the step, in mm)}$$
18 Standard Tolerance Grades (IT Grades)
Grade RangeCountIT MagnitudePrimary Application
IT01, IT02FinestSlip gauges, reference standards
IT1 – IT44Very fineHigh-precision gauges, plug gauges
IT5 – IT737i · 10i · 16iPrecision engineering fits
IT8 – IT11425i · 40i · 64i · 100iGeneral running and sliding fits
IT12 – IT165CoarsestSheet metal, castings, flame cutting
$$\text{IT5}=7i, \quad \text{IT6}=10i, \quad \text{IT7}=16i, \quad \text{IT8}=25i, \quad \text{IT9}=40i$$ $$\text{IT10}=64i, \quad \text{IT11}=100i, \quad \text{IT12}=160i$$
25 Fundamental Deviations
Holes (capital letters):
A B C D E F G H J JS K M N P R S T U V X Y Z ZA ZB ZC
H → EI = 0 (standard hole)
Shafts (lowercase letters):
a b c d e f g h j js k m n p r s t u v x y z za zb zc
h → es = 0 (standard shaft)
Designation Format
$$\underbrace{100}_{\text{Basic Size}} \;\underbrace{H7}_{\text{Hole: deviation H, IT7}} \;/\; \underbrace{f6}_{\text{Shaft: deviation f, IT6}}$$
IS:919 (Part I)–1993 (= ISO 286): 18 IT grades (IT01 to IT16), 25 fundamental deviations (A–ZC for holes, a–zc for shafts), range 1–500 mm.
Standard tolerance unit: \(i = 0.45\,\sqrt[3]{D} + 0.001\,D\;\mu\text{m}\).
Designated as: BasicSize × HoleSymbol / ShaftSymbol (e.g. 100 H7/f6).

09 Preferred Numbers — Full Explanation Theory
Question
What do you understand by preferred numbers? Explain fully.
Solution
Definition
Preferred numbers are a standardised series of numbers used to rationalise the sizes, capacities, and ratings of manufactured products. They are based on geometric progressions derived from powers of 10, defined in IS:1076 (equivalent to ISO 3 / Renard series).
Mathematical Basis — Renard Series
The ratio \(r\) between successive terms is constant (geometric progression). For a series with \(n\) steps per decade:
$$r = \sqrt[n]{10} = 10^{1/n}$$ $$\text{Each term:}\quad a_{k+1} = a_k \times r = a_k \times 10^{1/n}$$
SeriesSteps/DecadeCommon Ratio \(r\)Step SizeUse
R55\(\sqrt[5]{10} \approx 1.585\)~58.5%Motor power ratings
R1010\(\sqrt[10]{10} \approx 1.259\)~25.9%Belt pulley diameters, bolt sizes
R2020\(\sqrt[20]{10} \approx 1.122\)~12.2%Gear modules, spring wire
R4040\(\sqrt[40]{10} \approx 1.059\)~5.9%Drill bits, fine pitch series
R8080\(\sqrt[80]{10} \approx 1.030\)~3.0%Rarely used — very fine steps
R10 Series Example (1 to 10)
$$1.00,\quad 1.25,\quad 1.60,\quad 2.00,\quad 2.50,\quad 3.15,\quad 4.00,\quad 5.00,\quad 6.30,\quad 8.00,\quad 10.00$$ $$\text{Each term} \approx 1.259 \times \text{previous term}$$
Advantages
  • Reduces the number of different sizes — fewer tools, gauges, and raw materials
  • Ensures a uniform percentage step between sizes — logarithmically spaced
  • Enables rational extrapolation and interpolation within the series
  • Allows internationally consistent product ranges (IS:1076 ≡ ISO 3)
  • Simplifies stocking, inventory management, and part replacement
Applications in Mechanical Design
ApplicationPreferred Series
Gear module valuesR20 / R40
Belt pulley diametersR10 / R20
Standard bolt/screw sizesR10 / R20
Spring wire diametersR20 / R40
Electric motor power ratingsR5 / R10
Drill bit diametersR20 / R40
Preferred numbers (IS:1076 / ISO 3) are geometric series (R5, R10, R20, R40) based on \(r = \sqrt[n]{10}\), providing a constant percentage step between successive values.
They rationalise product sizes, reduce variety, simplify inventory, and ensure global interchangeability.
10 A basic shaft is one whose… MCQ
Question
A basic shaft is one whose — [Select correct answer]
Options
✘ (a) Lower deviation is zero
✔ (b) Upper deviation is zero
✘ (c) Lower and upper deviations are zero
✘ (d) None of these
Engineering Justification
A basic shaft is designated by the symbol h in the IS/ISO system. For shaft h, the fundamental deviation is the upper deviation (es), which is defined as zero.
$$\text{For basic shaft (h):} \quad \text{es} = 0$$ $$\text{Upper Limit of Shaft} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{es} = \text{Basic Size} + 0 = \text{Basic Size}$$ $$\text{Lower Limit of Shaft} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{ei} = \text{Basic Size} - \text{IT grade value}$$
$$\textbf{Example — Shaft 25h7:}$$ $$\text{es} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Upper Limit} = 25.000\;\text{mm (= Basic Size)}$$ $$\text{ei} = -21\;\mu\text{m} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Lower Limit} = 25.000 - 0.021 = 24.979\;\text{mm}$$
✔ Correct Answer: (b) Upper deviation is zero
For basic shaft (symbol h): \(\text{es} = 0\). The upper limit coincides with the basic size; all tolerance is applied downward (negative direction).
11 A basic hole is one whose… MCQ
Question
A basic hole is one whose — [Select correct answer]
Options
✔ (a) Lower deviation is zero
✘ (b) Upper deviation is zero
✘ (c) Lower and upper deviations are zero
✘ (d) None of these
Engineering Justification
A basic hole is designated by the symbol H in the IS/ISO system. For hole H, the fundamental deviation is the lower deviation (EI), which is defined as zero.
$$\text{For basic hole (H):} \quad \text{EI} = 0$$ $$\text{Lower Limit of Hole} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{EI} = \text{Basic Size} + 0 = \text{Basic Size}$$ $$\text{Upper Limit of Hole} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{ES} = \text{Basic Size} + \text{IT grade value}$$

✔ Correct Answer: (a) Lower deviation is zero
For basic hole (symbol H): \(\text{EI} = 0\). The lower limit coincides with the basic size; all tolerance is applied upward (positive direction).
12 100 H6/g5 means that the … (size) MCQ
Question
According to Indian standard specifications, 100 H6/g5 means that the — [Select correct answer]
Options
✘ (a) Actual size is 100 mm
✔ (b) Basic size is 100 mm
✘ (c) Difference between actual and basic size is 100 mm
✘ (d) None of the above
Engineering Justification — Decoding 100 H6/g5
$$\underbrace{100}_{\text{Basic Size = 100 mm}} \;\underbrace{H}_{\text{Hole (EI=0, hole basis)}} \underbrace{6}_{\text{IT Grade 6 → hole}} \;/\; \underbrace{g}_{\text{Shaft deviation g (small neg.)}} \underbrace{5}_{\text{IT Grade 5 → shaft}}$$
Why NOT (a): Actual size is the measured dimension of the manufactured part — it differs from the basic size by the actual deviation (in microns), and will never equal exactly 100.000 mm in practice.

Why NOT (c): The difference between actual size and basic size is the actual deviation, measured in micrometres (\(\mu\)m), never 100 mm.
✔ Correct Answer: (b) Basic size is 100 mm
The leading number in an IS/ISO fit designation always represents the basic size in millimetres — the exact reference from which all deviations and limits are calculated.

14 100 H6/g5 means that … (tolerance grades) MCQ
Question
According to Indian standard specification, 100 H6/g5 means that — [Select correct answer]
Options
✔ (a) Tolerance grade for the hole is 6 and for the shaft is 5
✘ (b) Tolerance grade for the shaft is 6 and for the hole is 5
✘ (c) Tolerance grade for the shaft is 4 to 8 and for the hole is 3 to 7
✘ (d) Tolerance grade for the hole is 4 to 8 and for the shaft is 3 to 7
Engineering Justification
$$\underbrace{100}_{\text{Basic Size}} \;\underbrace{H}_{\text{Capital = HOLE}} \underbrace{6}_{\text{IT Grade 6 → HOLE}} \;/\; \underbrace{g}_{\text{lowercase = SHAFT}} \underbrace{5}_{\text{IT Grade 5 → SHAFT}}$$
Convention: In IS/ISO notation, the hole designation (capital letter + number) always precedes the shaft designation (lowercase letter + number), separated by a forward slash.

Why NOT (b): Incorrectly reverses hole and shaft. H (capital) is always the hole; g (lowercase) is always the shaft.

Why NOT (c) and (d): A single fit designation specifies exact IT grades — not ranges. The grades are fixed single numbers (6 for hole, 5 for shaft).
$$\text{Hole} \;\rightarrow\; H\mathbf{6}\;:\;\text{IT grade 6 applies to the hole}$$ $$\text{Shaft} \;\rightarrow\; g\mathbf{5}\;:\;\text{IT grade 5 applies to the shaft}$$
✔ Correct Answer: (a) Tolerance grade for the hole is 6 and for the shaft is 5
In \(100\,H6/g5\): capital \(H\) + number \(6\) → Hole IT6; lowercase \(g\) + number \(5\) → Shaft IT5. Hole designation always precedes shaft designation in IS/ISO notation.
